Let me explain "False Security Sense" simply:
The Scenario:
A customer walks into a shop. A scammer has pasted a fake PhonePe QR sticker over the real one.
The customer opens your app (QR Guard) to scan it.
What Your Code Does Right Now:
It scans the fake sticker.
It sees the UPI ID inside (e.g., scammer@oksbi).
It checks your database: "Has anyone reported this specific UPI ID before?"
Result: If no one reported it yet, your app shows a Green Tick or says "No threats detected."
The "False Security" Trap:
The customer thinks: "Great! QR Guard scanned it and said it's safe. I will pay."
They pay the money. The money goes to the scammer.
Why is this "False"?
Your app didn't say it was a legitimate shop owner.
Your app only said "We don't know this is bad yet."
But the user interprets "No Warning" as "Safe to Pay."
The Hard Truth in Your Code:
For any QR code NOT generated by your system (like standard PhonePe/GPay stickers):
You cannot verify if the shop owner is real.
You cannot kill-switch it.
You cannot guarantee safety.
You are only checking against a list of previously reported scams.
The Danger:
If a brand new scam starts today, your app will show "Safe" for the first 100 victims because no one has reported it yet. Those 100 people trusted your "Green Tick," but they were actually unprotected. That is the False Security Sense.
How We Fix This Logic (Crucial for Success):
We must change the UI for non-system QRs:
Never show a Green Tick for external QRs.
Show a Yellow Warning: "This is a standard QR. We cannot verify the owner. Proceed with caution."
Only show Green Tick for QRs generated by YOUR system (where you verified the owner).
Brother, if we don't fix this distinction, users might blame your app when they get scammed on a normal QR, thinking your "Green Tick" guaranteed safety. We must be honest with the user: "We guarantee safety ONLY for QR Guard Verified QRs."
Does this make sense? This distinction is the difference between a useful tool and a liability.
